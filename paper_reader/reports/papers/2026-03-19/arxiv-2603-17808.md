# EVA: Aligning Video World Models with Executable Robot Actions via Inverse Dynamics Rewards

## Research Questions

- How can video world models for robotics be aligned to generate physically executable trajectories that respect kinematic constraints?
- Can an inverse dynamics model (IDM) be repurposed as a reward model to penalize executability violations during video generation training?
- What is the impact of IDM-based reward alignment on downstream robotic task execution success rates?

## How This Paper Solves It

The paper addresses the "executability gap" in video world models for robotics: while generated videos may look visually plausible, they often contain kinematic violations (arm deformations, impossible joint movements) that produce unstable robot actions when decoded by inverse dynamics models. The key insight is to train an IDM on real robot data and repurpose it as a reward model that evaluates generated videos through their implied action sequences, penalizing non-smooth motions and embodiment constraint violations. This creates a dense training signal that aligns video generation with physical feasibility without requiring expensive rejection sampling at inference time.

## Key Contributions

- Identifies and formalizes the "executability gap" between visually coherent video rollouts and physically executable robot actions
- Proposes EVA framework that uses IDM-derived rewards to align video world models via reinforcement learning post-training
- Develops IDM-based reward function that penalizes velocity/acceleration/jerk violations and out-of-bound actions while encouraging smooth motions
- Demonstrates improved kinematic plausibility (+20.9%) and task success rates (52.6% vs 46.2%) on RoboTwin benchmark and real robot experiments

## Key Formulas

### IDM-based Penalty Function

$$
\mathcal{P}(A) = \lambda_j\mathcal{P}_j + \lambda_\alpha\mathcal{P}_\alpha + \lambda_{v\text{-lim}}\mathcal{P}_{\text{vel}} + \lambda_{a\text{-lim}}\mathcal{P}_{\text{acc}}
$$

Total penalty P(A) combines jerk penalty P_j, acceleration penalty P_α, velocity limit violations P_vel, and acceleration limit violations P_acc, each weighted by lambda coefficients. This penalty is computed from IDM-predicted action sequences and captures kinematic implausibility.

### Executability Reward

$$
R(V) = \left(1 + \frac{\mathcal{P}(A)}{P_0}\right)^{-\gamma}
$$

Reward R(V) for video V is computed by mapping penalty P(A) into bounded reward using scale P_0 and decay rate γ. Higher penalties (more kinematic violations) result in lower rewards, providing training signal for GRPO alignment.

## Limitations of the Proposed Solution

- Reward focuses only on kinematic feasibility and smoothness, not contact dynamics like forces, friction, or torques critical for precision manipulation
- Diffusion-based video generation is computationally expensive, limiting applicability to high-frequency reactive control
- May exhibit reward hacking behaviors during prolonged GRPO training, requiring careful checkpoint selection and early stopping
- IDM spatial softmax architecture is crucial but may not generalize to all robot morphologies or action spaces

## Conclusions

- EVA improves kinematic plausibility by +20.9% and achieves 83.8% perfect execution rate on human evaluation
- Task success rates improve from 46.2% to 52.6% on RoboTwin benchmark with consistent gains across contact-rich tasks
- Real-world experiments show 64.0% vs 52.0% success on seen tasks and 60.0% vs 42.0% on out-of-distribution tasks
- IDM achieves 89.52% execution success rate on ground-truth demonstrations, validating its reliability as a reward model

## General Limitations

- Reward focuses only on kinematic feasibility and smoothness, not contact dynamics like forces, friction, or torques critical for precision manipulation
- Diffusion-based video generation is computationally expensive, limiting applicability to high-frequency reactive control
- May exhibit reward hacking behaviors during prolonged GRPO training, requiring careful checkpoint selection and early stopping
- IDM spatial softmax architecture is crucial but may not generalize to all robot morphologies or action spaces

## Abstract

Video generative models are increasingly used as world models for robotics, where a model generates a future visual rollout conditioned on the current observation and task instruction, and an inverse dynamics model (IDM) converts the generated frames into executable robot actions. However, current video world models lack explicit executability constraints. As a result, visually coherent rollouts may still violate rigid-body and kinematic consistency, producing unstable or infeasible control commands when decoded by an IDM. We refer to this mismatch between visual generation and physically executable control as the executability gap. While this gap can be mitigated at inference time using techniques such as rejection sampling, such approaches are inefficient due to the high cost of video generation. In this paper, we leverage the executability gap as a training signal and introduce Executable Video Alignment (EVA), a reinforcement-learning post-training framework for aligning video world models. EVA trains an inverse dynamics model on real robot trajectories and repurposes it as a reward model that evaluates generated videos through the action sequences they induce, encouraging smooth motions measured by velocity, acceleration, and jerk while penalizing actions that violate embodiment constraints. Importantly, the reward remains informative even when generated videos contain severe visual artifacts, since such artifacts typically translate into unstable or out-of-bound actions. Experiments on the RoboTwin benchmark and a real bimanual robot show that EVA reduces embodiment-specific artifacts in generated rollouts and improves downstream task execution success.
